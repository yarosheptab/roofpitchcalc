If you've ever stared up at a roof and wondered how steep it actually is — or needed that number for a contractor, a permit, or a materials quote — you're in the right place. Calculating roof pitch isn't complicated once you understand what you're measuring. This guide walks through four practical methods, from pencil-and-paper math to a quick online calculator.

## What Is Roof Pitch, and Why Does It Matter?

**Roof pitch** is the ratio of how much a roof rises vertically for every 12 inches it runs horizontally. A roof that rises 6 inches for every 12 inches of horizontal distance has a **6:12 pitch**.

Knowing your pitch matters for several real-world reasons:

- **Material selection** — most asphalt shingles require at least a 4:12 pitch; low-slope materials (TPO, modified bitumen) are needed below that
- **Permit applications** — building departments often ask for pitch on permit drawings
- **Insurance claims** — steeper roofs can affect replacement cost calculations
- **Contractor communication** — giving a roofer the correct pitch avoids measurement errors and wrong material orders

---

## Method 1: Rise and Run Formula

This is the foundational math method. It works whether you're on the roof or doing desktop planning.

**The formula:** Pitch = (Rise ÷ Run) × 12

**Rise** is the vertical distance from the top of the wall plate to the peak of the roof. **Run** is the horizontal distance from the outer wall to the center of the roof (half the total span).

### Step-by-Step Example

1. Measure the **run** — say the building is 20 feet wide, so the run is 10 feet (120 inches).
2. Measure the **rise** — the roof peaks 5 feet (60 inches) above the wall plate.
3. Plug into the formula: Pitch = (60 ÷ 120) × 12 = **6:12**

The result tells you: for every 12 inches of horizontal travel, this roof rises 6 inches.

### Working in Inches vs. Feet

You can use either unit, as long as you're consistent. Rise and run both in inches, or both in feet — the ratio stays the same because the 12 is a scaling factor, not a unit conversion.

## Method 2: Using a Level and Tape Measure

This is the go-to physical measurement method. You don't need to climb to the peak — you can do it from the attic or right on the roof surface.

### Tools Needed

- A standard 2-foot carpenter's level (or a torpedo level)
- A tape measure

### Steps

1. Hold the level **horizontally** against the roof surface, with one end touching the roof.
2. Mark a point **exactly 12 inches** along the level from where it touches the roof.
3. At that 12-inch mark, measure straight **down** to the roof surface.
4. That vertical measurement, in inches, **is your pitch number.**

For example: if the gap from the bottom of the level to the roof surface at the 12-inch mark is 5 inches, your pitch is **5:12**.

This method works best when done from inside the attic on the underside of the rafters — you get a clean, flat surface to work with and you're not dealing with shingles.

## Method 3: Smartphone App

Modern smartphones have the sensors to measure roof angle directly. The iOS **Measure** app includes a built-in level function. Android users can find free clinometer apps (search "clinometer" or "bubble level") in the Play Store.

### How to Use It

1. Open the level/clinometer function in the app.
2. Place your phone **flat against the roof surface** (or against a straight board laid on the roof).
3. Read the **angle in degrees** displayed on screen.
4. Convert the angle to pitch using this formula:

**Pitch = tan(angle°) × 12**

### Quick Conversion Example

Your phone reads **26.6 degrees.**

Pitch = tan(26.6°) × 12 = 0.501 × 12 ≈ **6:12**

Most scientific calculators and phone calculators have a tan function. This method is accurate to within a fraction of a pitch unit when your phone is lying flat and the surface is consistent.

## What If I Only Have the Angle in Degrees?

Sometimes a drawing or spec sheet gives you a roof angle in degrees instead of a pitch ratio. Convert it with:

**Pitch = tan(degrees × π ÷ 180) × 12**

Or more practically, just use the tan key on a calculator:

- tan(18.4°) × 12 = **4:12**
- tan(26.6°) × 12 = **6:12**
- tan(33.7°) × 12 = **8:12**
- tan(45°) × 12 = **12:12**

| 6:12  | 26.6°           | Common residential |
| 8:12  | 33.7°           | Moderately steep |
| 12:12 | 45°             | Steep / cottage style |

If your calculated pitch or angle falls outside these rough benchmarks for a typical house, double-check your measurements before ordering materials.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Measuring from the wrong point.** Rise must be measured from the top of the wall plate (the framing), not from the finished ceiling or the floor. Adding ceiling thickness to your rise will give you an inflated pitch number.

**Confusing full span with half-span.** The "run" in the pitch formula is always **half** the total building width (from one outer wall to the ridge). If a house is 24 feet wide, the run is 12 feet. Using 24 feet doubles your run and cuts your calculated pitch in half.

**Measuring on the shingles instead of the framing.** Shingles add a small but real thickness, and on older roofs they may be uneven. For the most accurate result, measure on the rafter itself or use the inside-attic method described above.

## Key Takeaways

- **Roof pitch = (rise ÷ run) × 12** — learn this one formula and every other method flows from it.
- The level-and-tape method is the most reliable for on-site measurement without special tools.
- Smartphone clinometers work well in a pinch; just remember to convert degrees to pitch using tan(angle°) × 12.
- An [online roof pitch calculator](/) handles the math instantly and adds rafter length as a bonus output.
- Watch out for the two classic mistakes: using full span instead of half-span, and measuring from the wrong reference point.

Once you have the pitch number, you're equipped to choose the right roofing material, fill out a permit application accurately, and talk to contractors in the same language they use every day.
